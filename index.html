School-Assessed Coursework 1 — Operations Management
Student: James Kowalski · Class A · Outcome 3 · Total: 30 marks · Reading + writing time: 60 min
Question 14 marks
Define operations management and outline its relationship to the achievement of business objectives.

Operations management is the process of transforming inputs such as raw materials, labour and capital into finished outputs, being the goods and services a business sells. It includes planning, organising and controlling the activities involved in production.

Operations management relates to business objectives because efficient operations lower the cost per unit and reduce waste, which helps a business achieve objectives such as making a profit and increasing market share. For example, a manufacturer that runs its operations efficiently can sell at a competitive price while still earning a healthy margin.

Question 26 marks
Explain two technological strategies a manufacturing business could implement to improve the efficiency and effectiveness of its operations.

One technological strategy is the use of robotics on automated production lines. Robotics improve efficiency because they operate continuously without breaks and produce output faster and more consistently than human workers, reducing the time taken per unit. They improve effectiveness by reducing human error, leading to more consistent product quality.

A second strategy is artificial intelligence (AI) used for demand forecasting and quality inspection. AI analyses large volumes of data to predict demand more accurately, reducing overproduction and waste (efficiency). AI-powered machine vision can also detect defects faster and more reliably than manual inspection, improving the effectiveness of quality control.

Question 38 marks
Analyse how a materials strategy and a waste minimisation strategy could be used together to improve the operations of a manufacturing business.

A materials strategy such as Just In Time (JIT) involves ordering and receiving materials only as they are needed in production. This improves operations by reducing the level of inventory held, lowering storage and holding costs and freeing up working capital. Because materials arrive only as required, there is less risk of stock becoming obsolete. However, JIT relies heavily on reliable suppliers — any delivery delay can halt production entirely.

A waste minimisation strategy such as lean management focuses on eliminating all forms of waste — overproduction, waiting, excess motion and defects. Removing activities that do not add value improves efficiency and lowers cost, and the continuous-improvement (kaizen) culture keeps employees involved in finding ongoing efficiencies.

Used together, the two strategies complement each other: JIT attacks inventory waste while lean management attacks process waste, producing a leaner, lower-cost operation overall. The main limitation is that both depend on supplier reliability and a significant cultural shift to work effectively.

Question 412 marks
Evaluate the effectiveness of Total Quality Management (TQM) as a strategy to improve the operations of a business. In your response, refer to a relevant business.

Total Quality Management (TQM) is a quality strategy in which every employee, across all areas of the business, takes responsibility for maintaining and improving quality, with a continuous focus on meeting customer needs.

A key strength of TQM is that it builds quality into every stage of production rather than relying on inspection at the end. This reduces defects and the costs of waste and rework. Because it involves all staff, it can also lift motivation and engagement, as workers feel ownership over quality. Over the long term, consistently high quality strengthens reputation and customer loyalty — supporting objectives such as growing market share. Toyota's use of TQM principles, for instance, has helped it build a global reputation for reliability.

However, TQM has real limitations. It is resource-intensive, requiring significant investment in staff training and a cultural shift across the whole organisation, which is costly and slow. The benefits are mostly long-term, which can frustrate managers seeking immediate gains, and it depends on sustained commitment from leadership — if that wavers, the strategy is unlikely to succeed.

Overall, TQM can be a highly effective operations strategy, particularly for businesses competing on quality and pursuing long-term improvement. Its effectiveness, though, depends on adequate resourcing, sustained commitment and a willingness to invest in cultural change. For a business chasing short-term cost cuts, a more targeted strategy may be more appropriate.

TQM makes quality everyone's responsibility and focuses on continuous improvement. It reduces defects and builds customer loyalty over time, supporting market-share objectives. Overall it suits businesses competing on quality, but a business needing fast cost savings might choose a more targeted approach.
